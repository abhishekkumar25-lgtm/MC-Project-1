Double and Triple Integration

AIM: To compute Double and Triple Integrals numerically and analytically

This experiment provides a comprehensive understanding of methods for evaluating double and triple integrals — fundamental techniques in multi-variable calculus and numerical methods — with support for both constant and variable limits of integration.

Understanding iterated integration, changing the order of integration, and geometric interpretations are crucial skills for engineering, physics, and data science applications.

Learning Objectives:

  • Understand double integrals ∬f(x,y) dA and their geometric meaning (volume under a surface)
  • Understand triple integrals ∭f(x,y,z) dV and their physical interpretations
  • Apply iterated integration with both constant and variable limits
  • Change the order of integration using Fubini's theorem
  • Solve integrals over non-rectangular regions using variable limits

Applications:

  • Engineering: Area, volume, mass, center of mass, moment of inertia
  • Physics: Flux, charge distribution, probability density functions
  • Computer Graphics: Rendering equations, light transport
  • Data Science: Multivariate probability distributions, marginal densities
  • Machine Learning: Expectation over joint distributions

Theory of Double and Triple Integration

Double Integrals

A double integral extends the concept of a single integral to functions of two variables. Given f(x,y) defined over a region R in the xy-plane, the double integral is:

∬R f(x,y) dA = limn→∞ Σ f(xᵢ,yⱼ) ΔA

Geometrically, if f(x,y) ≥ 0, the double integral gives the volume of the solid between the surface z = f(x,y) and the region R in the xy-plane.

Rectangular Region (constant limits)

∫ab ∫cd f(x,y) dy dx

Inner integral: integrate f(x,y) w.r.t. y from c to d
Outer integral: integrate result w.r.t. x from a to b

Non-Rectangular Region (variable limits)

When the region is bounded by curves, limits of the inner integral depend on the outer variable:

∫ab ∫g₁(x)g₂(x) f(x,y) dy dx

Example: ∫₀¹ ∫₀ˣ (x + y) dy dx
Inner limits: y from 0 to x (variable upper limit)
Outer limits: x from 0 to 1 (constant)

Triple Integrals

A triple integral extends the concept to functions of three variables f(x,y,z) over a 3D region E:

∭E f(x,y,z) dV = ∫∫∫ f(x,y,z) dz dy dx

Constant Limits (Rectangular Box)

∫ab ∫cd ∫ef f(x,y,z) dz dy dx

Example: ∫₀¹ ∫₀¹ ∫₀¹ xyz dz dy dx = 1/8

Variable Limits

Limits can be functions of outer variables — the innermost variable has limits depending on both outer variables:

∫ab ∫g₁(x)g₂(x) ∫h₁(x,y)h₂(x,y) f(x,y,z) dz dy dx

Example: ∫₀¹ ∫₀ˣ ∫₀ˣ⁺ʸ dz dy dx
Inner z-limits: 0 to x+y (function of x and y)

Changing the Order of Integration

By Fubini's Theorem, for continuous functions on a bounded region, the order of integration can be reversed. This is often useful when one order is easier to evaluate.

Original: ∫₀¹ ∫ₓ¹ f(x,y) dy dx
Region R: 0 ≤ x ≤ 1, x ≤ y ≤ 1

Reversed: ∫₀¹ ∫₀ʸ f(x,y) dx dy
Region R: 0 ≤ y ≤ 1, 0 ≤ x ≤ y

Coordinate Systems

  • Cartesian (x,y,z): Standard; dV = dx dy dz
  • Polar (r,θ): For circular regions; dA = r dr dθ
  • Cylindrical (r,θ,z): For cylindrical regions; dV = r dz dr dθ
  • Spherical (ρ,φ,θ): For spherical regions; dV = ρ² sin(φ) dρ dφ dθ

Posttest — Practical Problem Solving

Complete this posttest to evaluate your understanding of double and triple integration.

Problem 1: Evaluate ∫₀¹ ∫₀¹ (x + y) dy dx
Problem 2: What is ∫₀¹ ∫₀ˣ y dy dx? (variable upper limit x)
Problem 3: The volume of the solid under z = 1 and above the unit square [0,1]×[0,1] is:
Problem 4: Evaluate ∫₀¹ ∫₀¹ ∫₀¹ xyz dz dy dx
Problem 5: In ∫₀¹ ∫₀ˣ ∫₀ˣ⁺ʸ dz dy dx, what is the upper limit of z in terms of the outer variables?

Solutions to Posttest Problems

Problem 1 Solution:

Inner: ∫₀¹ (x + y) dy = [xy + y²/2]₀¹ = x + 1/2
Outer: ∫₀¹ (x + 1/2) dx = [x²/2 + x/2]₀¹ = 1/2 + 1/2 = 1

Problem 2 Solution:

Inner: ∫₀ˣ y dy = [y²/2]₀ˣ = x²/2
Outer: ∫₀¹ x²/2 dx = [x³/6]₀¹ = 1/6

Problem 3 Solution:

∫₀¹ ∫₀¹ 1 dy dx = ∫₀¹ [y]₀¹ dx = ∫₀¹ 1 dx = 1

Problem 4 Solution:

Innermost: ∫₀¹ xyz dz = xy[z²/2]₀¹ = xy/2
Middle: ∫₀¹ xy/2 dy = x/2 · [y²/2]₀¹ = x/4
Outer: ∫₀¹ x/4 dx = [x²/8]₀¹ = 1/8

Problem 5 Solution:

In ∫₀¹ ∫₀ˣ ∫₀x+y dz dy dx, the z-limits go from 0 to x+y, which is a function of both outer variables x and y. This is a variable limit depending on both x and y.
